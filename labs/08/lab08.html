Gain and phase of continuous time system's transfer function: the Bode plot¶

Find the gain and phase angle for angular frequencies 0 rad/s (d.c.), 2 rad/s and for very large frequencies if the system is characterised by the transfer function $$ H(s) = \dfrac{s+1}{s+2}\rightarrow |H(j\omega)|=\sqrt{\dfrac{\omega^2 + 1}{\omega^2 + 4}}\ ,\quad \angle H(j\omega)=\arctan\frac{\omega}{1} - \arctan\frac{\omega}{2} $$

$\omega = 0$ rad/s $\rightarrow$ {Gain} = 0.5, phase angle = 0$^\circ$ $\omega = 2$ rad/s $\rightarrow$ {Gain} =$\sqrt{\frac{5}{8}}\approx 0,79$, phase angle = $\arctan 2 - \arctan 1 = 18.4^\circ$ $\omega \rightarrow\infty$ rad/s $\rightarrow$ {Gain} $\rightarrow$ 1, phase angle = $\frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{2}=0^\circ$

from pylab import *
from scipy import signal
num, den = [1, 1], [1, 2]
o, gain, phase = signal.bode((num, den))
def gain_theory(o):
    return 20*log10(sqrt(o**2 + 1)/sqrt(o**2+4))
def phase_theory(o):
    return (arctan(o) - arctan(o/2))*180/pi
plot(o, gain           , linewidth=7, alpha=0.5, label='gain numeric') # o is omega in rad/s, gain in decibels
plot(o, phase          , linewidth=7, alpha=0.5, label='phase numeric') # phase in degrees
plot(o, gain_theory (o), 'k-'                  , label='gain theory'  )
plot(o, phase_theory(o), 'b-'                  , label='phase theory' )
plot([0], [gain_theory (0)], 'ro', [2], [gain_theory (2)], 'go')
plot([0], [phase_theory(0)], 'ro', [2], [phase_theory(2)], 'go')
grid()
legend()
print(10**(gain_theory(0)/20) , phase_theory (0),'deg', 10**(gain_theory(2)/20), phase_theory(2),'deg')

Signature: signal.bode(system, w=None, n=100)
Docstring:
Calculate Bode magnitude and phase data of a continuous-time system.

Parameters
----------
system : an instance of the LTI class or a tuple describing the system.
    The following gives the number of elements in the tuple and
    the interpretation:

        * 1 (instance of `lti`)
        * 2 (num, den)
        * 3 (zeros, poles, gain)
        * 4 (A, B, C, D)

w : array_like, optional
    Array of frequencies (in rad/s). Magnitude and phase data is calculated
    for every value in this array. If not given a reasonable set will be
    calculated.
n : int, optional
    Number of frequency points to compute if `w` is not given. The `n`
    frequencies are logarithmically spaced in an interval chosen to
    include the influence of the poles and zeros of the system.

Returns
-------
w : 1D ndarray
    Frequency array [rad/s]
mag : 1D ndarray
    Magnitude array [dB]
phase : 1D ndarray
    Phase array [deg]

Notes
-----
If (num, den) is passed in for ``system``, coefficients for both the
numerator and denominator should be specified in descending exponent
order (e.g. ``s^2 + 3s + 5`` would be represented as ``[1, 3, 5]``).

.. versionadded:: 0.11.0

Examples
--------
>>> from scipy import signal
>>> import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

>>> sys = signal.TransferFunction([1], [1, 1])
>>> w, mag, phase = signal.bode(sys)

>>> plt.figure()
>>> plt.semilogx(w, mag)    # Bode magnitude plot
>>> plt.figure()
>>> plt.semilogx(w, phase)  # Bode phase plot
>>> plt.show()

The $z$ transform¶

Power series recap:¶

$$ S_N = \sum_{n=0}^{N} r^n $$

Multiply by $r$: $$ rS_N = \sum_{n=0}^{N} r^{n+1} = \sum_{n=1}^{N+1} r^n=S_{N} + r^{N+1} - 1 $$

$$ (1-r)S_N = 1-r^{N+1} $$$$ \boxed{ S_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N}r^n=\frac{1-r^{N+1}}{1-r} } $$

If $|r|<1$, then $$ r^{N+1}\to 0, $$

so $$ \boxed{ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}r^n=\frac{1}{1-r}, \qquad |r|<1 } $$

Exercise¶

Calculate the $z$-transform of $$ u[n] = \begin{cases} 0\ , & n < 0\\ 1\ , & n\geqslant 0 \end{cases} $$

Solution:¶

$$ U(z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} u[n]\,z^{-n} =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} z^{-n} $$$$ U(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (z^{-1})^n =\frac{1}{1-z^{-1}}, \quad |z|>1 $$$$ \boxed{ U(z)=\frac{1}{1-z^{-1}}=\frac{z}{z-1}, \quad \text{ROC: } |z|>1 } $$

Exercise¶

Show that if sampled at intervals $T$ the $z$-transform of $x(t) = t$ is $\frac{Tz}{(z-1)^2}$;

Solution¶

Sampling $x(t)=t$ at intervals $T$ gives $$ x[n]=x(nT)=nT,\qquad n\ge 0. $$

Therefore its $z$-transform is $$ X(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} nT z^{-n} =T\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n z^{-n}. $$

Start from the geometric series: $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n=\frac{1}{1-r},\qquad |r|<1. $$

Differentiate with respect to $r$: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n r^{n-1} = \frac{1}{(1-r)^2}. $$

Multiply by $r$: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n r^n = \frac{r}{(1-r)^2}. $$

Now set $$ r=z^{-1}. $$

Then $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n z^{-n} = \frac{z^{-1}}{(1-z^{-1})^2}. $$

Hence $$ X(z) = T\frac{z^{-1}}{(1-z^{-1})^2}. $$

Finally, $$ T\frac{z^{-1}}{(1-z^{-1})^2} = T\frac{z^{-1}}{\left(\frac{z-1}{z}\right)^2} = T\frac{z^{-1}z^2}{(z-1)^2} = \boxed{ \frac{Tz}{(z-1)^2} } $$

with ROC: $$ \boxed{|z|>1.} $$

Exercise¶

Show that if sampled at intervals $T$ the $z$-transform of $x(t) =e^{-at}$ is $\frac{z}{z-e^{-aT}}$

Solution¶

Sampling $x(t)=e^{-at}$ at intervals $T$ gives $$ x[n]=x(nT)=e^{-anT}=\left(e^{-aT}\right)^n,\qquad n\ge 0. $$

Therefore its $z$-transform is $$ X(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x[n]z^{-n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(e^{-aT}\right)^n z^{-n}. $$

Rewrite the terms: $$ X(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(e^{-aT}z^{-1}\right)^n. $$

Using the geometric series formula, $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n=\frac{1}{1-r},\qquad |r|<1, $$

with $$ r=e^{-aT}z^{-1}, $$

we get $$ X(z)=\frac{1}{1-e^{-aT}z^{-1}}. $$

Equivalently, $$ \boxed{ X(z)=\frac{z}{z-e^{-aT}} } $$

with ROC: $$ \boxed{ |z|>e^{-aT} } $$

Exercise¶

Show that if sampled at intervals $T$, the $z$-transform of $x(t)=\sin \omega t$ is $$ X_{\sin}(z)=\frac{z\sin\omega T}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}, $$ and the $z$-transform of $x(t)=\cos \omega t$ is $$ X_{\cos}(z)=\frac{z(z-\cos\omega T)}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}. $$

Solution¶

Using the previous result for $x(t)=e^{-at}$, we may write $$ \mathcal{Z}\left\{e^{-anT}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{-aT}}. $$

Now take $a=-i\omega$. Then $$ e^{-aT}=e^{i\omega T}, $$ so $$ \mathcal{Z}\left\{e^{i\omega nT}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}. $$

$$ \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}=\frac{z(z-e^{-i\omega T})}{(z-e^{i\omega T})(z-e^{-i\omega T})}. $$

Since $$ (z-e^{i\omega T})(z-e^{-i\omega T})=z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1,$$ we get $$\frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}=\frac{z(z-e^{-i\omega T})}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}.$$

Now use $$ e^{-i\omega T}=\cos\omega T-i\sin\omega T. $$ Therefore $$ \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}=\frac{z(z-\cos\omega T+i\sin\omega T)}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}. $$ But $$ e^{i\omega nT}=\cos(\omega nT)+i\sin(\omega nT), $$ so the real part gives the cosine transform: $$ \boxed{X_{\cos}(z)=\frac{z(z-\cos\omega T)}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}} $$ and the imaginary part gives the sine transform: $$ \boxed{X_{\sin}(z)=\frac{z\sin\omega T}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1}} $$

The ROC is $$ \boxed{|z|>1} $$ for the causal sampled sequences.

Alternative Solution¶

$$ \mathcal{Z}\left\{e^{i\omega nT}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}. \qquad \mathcal{Z}\left\{e^{-i\omega nT}\right\} = \frac{z}{z-e^{-i\omega T}}. $$

Using Euler's formulas, $$ \cos(\omega nT)=\frac{e^{i\omega nT}+e^{-i\omega nT}}{2}, \qquad \sin(\omega nT)=\frac{e^{i\omega nT}-e^{-i\omega nT}}{2i}. $$

Therefore $$ X_{\cos}(z) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}

\frac{z}{z-e^{-i\omega T}} \right). $$

Putting the terms over a common denominator, $$ X_{\cos}(z) = \frac{z}{2} \frac{ (z-e^{-i\omega T})+(z-e^{i\omega T}) }{ (z-e^{i\omega T})(z-e^{-i\omega T}) }. $$

The numerator becomes $$ (z-e^{-i\omega T})+(z-e^{i\omega T}) = 2z-\left(e^{i\omega T}+e^{-i\omega T}\right) = 2z-2\cos\omega T. $$

The denominator becomes $$ (z-e^{i\omega T})(z-e^{-i\omega T}) = z^2-z\left(e^{i\omega T}+e^{-i\omega T}\right)+1 = z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1. $$

Hence $$ \boxed{ X_{\cos}(z) = \frac{z(z-\cos\omega T)}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1} } $$

Similarly, $$ X_{\sin}(z)= \frac{1}{2i} \left( \frac{z}{z-e^{i\omega T}}-\frac{z}{z-e^{-i\omega T}} \right). $$

Putting the terms over a common denominator, $$ X_{\sin}(z) = \frac{z}{2i} \frac{ (z-e^{-i\omega T})-(z-e^{i\omega T}) }{ (z-e^{i\omega T})(z-e^{-i\omega T}) }. $$

The numerator becomes $$ (z-e^{-i\omega T})-(z-e^{i\omega T}) = e^{i\omega T}-e^{-i\omega T} = 2i\sin\omega T. $$

Therefore $$ \boxed{ X_{\sin}(z) = \frac{z\sin\omega T}{z^2-2z\cos\omega T+1} } $$

Exercise¶

If the input to a system, having a transfer function of $ 1 + 2z^{-1} - z^{-2} $, is a discrete unit step, show that the first four terms of the output sequence are 1, 3, 2, 2.

Solution 1¶

$$ H(z)=1+2z^{-1}-z^{-2} $$

$ h[n]=\delta[n]+2\delta[n-1]-\delta[n-2] $, i.e., $h=\{1, 2, -1\}$ (impulse response)

The input is the discrete unit step:

$x[n]=u[n]$, i.e., $x=\{\dots, 0, 0, \textbf{1}, 1, \dots\}$ (impulse response)

For an LTI system,

$$ y[n]=h[n]*x[n] $$

Therefore,

$$ \begin{bmatrix} y[0]\\ y[1]\\ y[2]\\ y[3]\\ \vdots \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ 2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ -1 & 2 & 1 & 0 & \dots\\ 0 & -1 & 2 & 1& \dots\\ & &\vdots & & \ddots \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ 1\\ 1\\ \vdots \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 3\\ 2\\ 2\\ \vdots \end{bmatrix}. $$

Solution 2¶

$$ H(z)=1+2z^{-1}-z^{-2} $$$$ x[n]=u[n] \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad X(z)=\frac{1}{1-z^{-1}}, \qquad |z|>1 $$$$ Y(z)=H(z)X(z) = \left(1+2z^{-1}-z^{-2}\right)\frac{1}{1-z^{-1}} $$$$ Y(z)= \frac{1+2z^{-1}-z^{-2}}{1-z^{-1}} $$

Now divide the two polynomials in powers of $z^{-1}$:

$$ \begin{array}{rrrrrr|l} 1 & +2z^{-1} & -z^{-2} & +0z^{-3} & +0z^{-4} & \cdots & 1-z^{-1} \\ \hline &&&&&& 1+3z^{-1}+2z^{-2}+2z^{-3}+2z^{-4}+\cdots \\[1mm] 1 & -z^{-1} \\ \hline & 3z^{-1} & -z^{-2} \\ & 3z^{-1} & -3z^{-2} \\ \hline && 2z^{-2} & +0z^{-3} \\ && 2z^{-2} & -2z^{-3} \\ \hline &&& 2z^{-3} & +0z^{-4} \\ &&& 2z^{-3} & -2z^{-4} \\ \hline &&&& 2z^{-4} & \cdots \end{array} $$

Therefore,

$$ Y(z)=1+3z^{-1}+2z^{-2}+2z^{-3}+2z^{-4}+\cdots $$

Since

$$ Y(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} y[n]z^{-n}, $$

we read the samples directly:

$$ y[0]=1,\qquad y[1]=3,\qquad y[2]=2,\qquad y[3]=2. $$

Hence the first four terms are

$$ \boxed{1,\ 3,\ 2,\ 2}. $$

Exercise¶

When a discrete signal ( 1, -2 ) is inputted to a processing system, the output sequence is ( 1, -5, 8, -4 ).

a. Derive the transfer function for the system.

b. Find the first three terms of the output sequence in response to the finite input sequence of ( 2, 2, 1 ).

Solution 1¶

a. $$ x[n]=\{1,-2\} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad X(z)=1-2z^{-1} $$

$$ y[n]=\{1,-5,8,-4\} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad Y(z)=1-5z^{-1}+8z^{-2}-4z^{-3} $$$$ H(z)=\frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{1-5z^{-1}+8z^{-2}-4z^{-3}}{1-2z^{-1}} $$

Now perform the division in powers of $z^{-1}$:

$$ \begin{array}{rrrrr|l} 1 & -5z^{-1} & +8z^{-2} & -4z^{-3} & +0z^{-4} & 1-2z^{-1}\\ \hline &&&&& 1-3z^{-1}+2z^{-2} \\[1mm] 1 & -2z^{-1} \\ \hline & -3z^{-1} & +8z^{-2} \\ & -3z^{-1} & +6z^{-2} \\ \hline && 2z^{-2} & -4z^{-3} \\ && 2z^{-2} & -4z^{-3} \\ \hline &&& 0 \end{array} $$

Therefore,

$$ \boxed{H(z)=1-3z^{-1}+2z^{-2}.} $$

b. $$ h[n]=\{1,-3,2\}, \qquad x[n]=\{2,2,1\}. $$

Using matrix-based convolution,

$$ y= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & -3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ 2\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -4\\ -1\\ 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Therefore, the first three terms are

$$ \boxed{2,\,-4,\,-1}. $$

Solution 2.¶

a.

$$ x[n]=\{1,-2\}, \qquad y[n]=\{1,-5,8,-4\}. $$

Assume the system impulse response has the form

$$ h[n]=\{h[0],h[1],h[2]\}. $$

Since

$$ y[n]=h[n]*x[n], $$

we can write the convolution in matrix form:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} y[0]\\ y[1]\\ y[2]\\ y[3] \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ -2 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & -2 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} h[0]\\ h[1]\\ h[2] \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -5\\ 8\\ -4 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Therefore,

$$ h[0]=1, $$$$ -2h[0]+h[1]=-5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad h[1]=-3, $$$$ -2h[1]+h[2]=8 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad h[2]=2. $$

Thus,

$$ h[n]=\{1,-3,2\}. $$

So the transfer function is

$$ \boxed{ H(z)=1-3z^{-1}+2z^{-2}. } $$

b.

$$ Y_1(z)=X_1(z)H(z), \qquad Y_2(z)=X_2(z)H(z). $$

Eliminating $H(z)$ gives

$$ \frac{Y_1(z)}{X_1(z)} = \frac{Y_2(z)}{X_2(z)}. $$

Therefore,

$$ X_1(z)Y_2(z)=X_2(z)Y_1(z). $$

In the time domain, multiplication in the $z$-domain corresponds to convolution:

$$ x_1[n]*y_2[n]=x_2[n]*y_1[n]. $$

For this problem,

$$ x_1[n]=\{1,-2\}, \qquad y_1[n]=\{1,-5,8,-4\}, \qquad x_2[n]=\{2,2,1\}. $$

On the right hand side since $x_2$ has length 3 and $y_1$ has length 4, the convolution $x_2*y_1$ has length 3+4-1=6.

$$ x_2*y_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2&0&0&0\\ 2&2&0&0\\ 1&2&2&0\\ 0&1&2&2\\ 0&0&1&2\\ 0&0&0&1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -5\\ 8\\ -4 \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -8\\ 7\\ 3\\ 0\\ -4 \end{bmatrix}.. $$

On the right hand side $x_1$ has length two so $y_2$ must have length 5 as 2 + 5 - 1=6.

Let

$$ \mathbf y_2= \begin{bmatrix} y_2[0]\\ y_2[1]\\ y_2[2]\\ y_2[3]\\ y_2[4] \end{bmatrix}. $$

Using the convolution matrix generated by $x_1=\{1,-2\}$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1&0&0&0&0\\ -2&1&0&0&0\\ 0&-2&1&0&0\\ 0&0&-2&1&0\\ 0&0&0&-2&1\\ 0&0&0&0&-2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} y_2[0]\\ y_2[1]\\ y_2[2]\\ y_2[3]\\ y_2[4] \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -8\\ 7\\ 3\\ 0\\ -4 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Solving by forward substitution:

$$ y_2[0]=2 $$$$ -2y_2[0]+y_2[1]=-8 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_2[1]=-4 $$$$ -2y_2[1]+y_2[2]=7 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_2[2]=-1 $$$$ -2y_2[2]+y_2[3]=3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_2[3]=1 $$$$ -2y_2[3]+y_2[4]=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_2[4]=2 $$

Therefore,

$$ \mathbf y_2= \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -4\\ -1\\ 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix}. $$

So the first three terms are

$$ \boxed{2,\,-4,\,-1}. $$
